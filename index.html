We remember music from movies almost as often as we recall particular scenes from movies; music has a unique ability to heighten the emotional impact of a motion picture. As early as the 1890s, before audible dialogue was introduced into film, a pianist, organist or small orchestra would accompany silent films. Today’s movie soundtracks feature a wide range of original compositions, prewritten classical pieces and numerous music genres including pop and rock. This article will explore a data set that contains prewritten classical music that has appeared in the movies since the 1930s. Hopefully this article will provide a bit more clarity on some of the top classical pieces that may have been recognizable but nameless in the past!

Top Classical Composers in the Movies 1930-2019

The top pieces of classical music in movies capture various emotions of the human experience- joy, relief, humor, celebration and adventure, just to name a few. The top composers for this list may come as no surprise- Mozart, Bach and Beethoven are considered to be the top classical composers of all time.

The number one piece of classical music used in the movies is The Blue Danube by Johann Strauss II. A total of 48 movie soundtracks contain this piece spanning from 1937 to 2015. For many early movies, The Blue Danube functions as a background waltz for dancing or scenes of beauty and weightlessness.

As the years go on, there seems to be a growing trend to use the music ironically or in juxtaposition to other elements of the movie scene such as dialogue or action.

How Often Do Movies Use Classical Music?

There are a few periods in time where classical music is used at a much higher frequency than in other years: one period lasts for the 1990s into the early 2000s and the other is the year 2015.

Many of the movies that contain the most pieces of classical music were released in the 1990s. This includes Immortal Beloved (25 pieces), Glenn Gould (22 pieces), He Got Game (19 pieces), Sour Grapes (13 pieces), Lorenzo’s Oil (12 pieces), Shine (12 Pieces), Hilary and Jackie (12 pieces), The Accompanist (11 pieces) and Anna Karenina (11 pieces). This piece from the Guardian describes the 90s as a time when movies “depict a simpler, more innocent time”. Could classical music be used as a way of depicting these ideals? Furthermore, while 2015 was a great year for the box office, it seems that for our data set it was just an outlier year for classical music in film.

Genre Breakdown for the Top Ten Pieces

Next, we can go more in depth with each piece in the top ten and explore the genre breakdown each individual piece. We have higher numbers for the areas of Drama, Comedy and Romance. In fact, the majority of the top ten pieces have the highest number of movies in the genre of comedy. The only two pieces that have a higher number of movies in Drama are The Blue Danube (which is closely ranked next to Comedy) and the Chopin Nocturne, which happens to be a very dramatic piece itself. It’s interesting to observe an uptick in Fantasy and Horror for the Toccata and Fugue piece.

Genre Distribution for the Movies with the Highest Number of Classical Pieces

This sankey chart displays the top ten movies that have the most classical music used it them, ranging from 25 pieces to 12 pieces. Many of these movies have to do with musical stories, such as the life and times of a particular composer or musician, or a group of musicians performing music together. The only exceptions for this are He Got Game, Lorenzo's Oil, and Sour Grapes. We see that the highest number of movies in a particular genre is for Drama.

Marriage Portrayed Throughout the Years

The second and third most popular classical pieces in movies are both used in weddings. The Lohengrin: Bridal Chorus is used as a processional as the bride walks down the aisle. Interestingly enough, the opera from which it originates, Lohengrin, does not use the chorus as a processional; it is actually used after the ceremony. A Midsummer Night’s Dream Wedding March is traditionally used as a recessional piece after newlyweds kiss and walk back down the aisle. The 43 movies that A Midsummer Night’s Dream Wedding March was used in were analyzed for content and show minimal changes over time among the wedding scenes: the scene typically consisted of a caucasian female walking down the aisle. A hypothesis before the research was that the wedding scenes would reflect changes in culture over time, such as race and sexuality. This turned out to not be the case. The very reason that this piece could be chosen is to symbolize the very traditional, conservative associations of marriage.
